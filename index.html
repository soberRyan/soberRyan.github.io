Reflections on the 12 Steps

Experiences, challenges, and personal growth

Step 1

Step 1 in AA is about admitting, deep down, that we are alcoholics. For me, that meant shifting from self-pity to an empowering realization that I get to be an alcoholic. Recognizing my addictive personality helped me focus my work on the remaining steps, establishing a connection with God, and letting go of long-held resentments, fears, anxieties, and depression. Some sponsors suggest making two lists—how alcohol rendered you powerless and how it made your life unmanageable—but my biggest hurdle was fully embracing that I was an alcoholic instead of forever searching for a way to drink “like a normal person.”

Step 2

Step 2 is about coming to believe that a power greater than ourselves can restore us to sanity. At first, I didn't want to admit that alcohol, drugs, and my behavior had driven me to insanity. Nor did I embrace the notion of God, believing Him akin to Santa Claus or the Tooth Fairy. Many in recovery define God in different ways—“group of drunks,” “good orderly direction,” or otherwise. Over time, I started to see God as Love and the universal energy present in everyone and everything. I believe God speaks through people, experiences, and particularly my intuition. This viewpoint took a long time to shape, and I work continuously to nurture it.

Step 3

Step 3 focuses on turning our will and our lives over to God as we understand Him. This was a tough step for me and one that I have to re-commit to daily. Each morning, I pray for guidance in my thoughts and actions—those being the essence of my will and my life. I believe God's will is for me to be the best version of myself and to help others. It's easy to slip back into “my will,” so I keep this step alive by praying, believing, and following that inner nudge toward the next right action.

Step 4

Step 4 involves taking a searching and fearless moral inventory of ourselves. While it was easy to dwell on my flaws internally, the real relief came when I wrote them down, prayed about them, and shared them with my sponsor. I also find value in revisiting Step 4 multiple times as I continue in sobriety. As more insights emerge—often when I’m helping others through their inventory—my own understanding deepens and evolves.

Step 5

Step 5 is about admitting to God, ourselves, and another human being the exact nature of our wrongs. It usually means reading through our Step 4 inventory with a sponsor, accompanied by prayer. This kind of honesty often lifts shame and guilt that can keep us stuck in the past.

Steps 6 and 7

Steps 6 and 7 deal with becoming entirely ready for God to remove our character defects, and then humbly asking Him to do so. They're closely connected, and it's easy to mix up their specific wording. In essence, Step 6 is about willingness, and Step 7 is about surrender—asking God for the help we need to truly let go of these defects.

Step 8

Step 8 involves listing all the people we've harmed and becoming willing to make amends to them. Just like Step 4, my list has grown over time as I remember more incidents or recognize new ways my actions affected others. Though it might sound overwhelming, it’s primarily a preparation step— Step 9 is where the actual amends take place.

Step 9

Step 9 is about making direct amends whenever possible, unless doing so would harm someone else. In my experience, amends I've made have always gone better than I feared and have helped restore or improve relationships. They often brought unexpected blessings and gave me a deeper understanding of both myself and the people I harmed.

Step 10

Step 10 instructs us to continue taking personal inventory and admit when we’re wrong without delay. This involves daily checks on my resentments, fears, or situations where I might owe an amends. Regularly returning to the previous steps—and helping others work through them—keeps me accountable and aware of my growth areas.

Step 11

Step 11 encourages prayer (talking to God) and meditation (listening to God). I don’t do this as consistently as I’d like, but I definitely notice a difference on days I start with prayer and meditation. I also view my daily thoughts as a form of prayer, so I monitor what I’m dwelling on. I like having free-flow, conversational prayers with God in my head, and I practice mindfulness by remaining present. Focusing on the past can lead to depression, while worrying about the future can trigger anxiety. Staying in the now keeps me more in tune with God’s presence.

Step 12

Step 12 is about carrying the message to other alcoholics and practicing these principles in all our affairs. It’s easier to keep the principles in play during meetings or while helping another person in recovery. The real challenge is in daily life, especially when I’m alone or at home. Showing up for meetings, doing service work, and supporting newcomers keeps me active in Step 12.
